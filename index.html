About What is a Julian Day?

The modern civil calendar is a solar calendar consisting of 365 days per year, with an additional leap day every four years (subject to a few century exceptions). While this system works well for everyday life, astronomical calculations require a continuous measure of time.

To achieve this, astronomers use the Julian Day (JD) — a single, continuously increasing count of the number of days that have elapsed since January 1, 4713 BC, at 12:00 UTC. Unlike calendar dates, the Julian Day has no months or years, making it ideal for precise calculations.

Every higher-level calculation in this tool — including local sidereal time, coordinate transformations, and planetary positions — begins by converting the selected date and time into a Julian Day. A commonly used reference is 2000 Jan 1, 12:00 UTC = JD 2451545.0, known as the J2000.0 epoch. Enter this date above to verify the calculation yourself.

About What is Local Sidereal Time?

While solar time tracks the Sun, sidereal time tracks the distant stars. Because Earth orbits the Sun, a solar day is about 4 minutes longer than a sidereal day — and it is sidereal time that tells a telescope where to look.

Greenwich Mean Sidereal Time (GMST) measures how far Earth has rotated relative to the stars since J2000.0. Adding the observer's longitude converts it to Local Sidereal Time (LST) — the right ascension currently crossing the meridian at that location. Any star whose right ascension equals the current LST is, at that moment, due south and at its highest point in the sky.
